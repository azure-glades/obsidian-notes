# Systems Theory of Management
Systems theory views and organization as a set of interrelated and interacting systems that work together to achieve the organization goals. Each system has its own activity, goals, and requirements.
- Each system requires its own manager, and these managers act as points of contact/interaction across many systems
- Activity of each system affects the activity of other systems
Key points:
1. A system is goal-oriented
2. A system has several sub-systems that are interrelated and interdependent
3. A system processes/transforms inputs to outputs
4. An organization is dynamic and open system. There are also closes systems.
5. Systems interact through boundaries (i.e responsibilities/capabilities of a system that separate it from other systems)
6. Different systems/subsystems interact and co-operate with each other for higher productivity (*synergy*).
7. Flow: Information, Material and energy flows through and between systems. There is usually a direction for this flow that determines the importance of different systems.
8. Feedback: Information/critic about a system's functioning that is given by an entity outside the system. It affects how systems decide and how operations are done. Generally, feedback is given by computers and supervisors to make a system/dept more productive
System interactions happen in three steps:
**Input -> Processing -> Output**
System has input from env and other systems
System operations, like work, management, etc process and transform the input
System gives outputs to env or to other systems

# Contingency/Situational Approach
The contingency approach is a management theory that emphasizes there is no single best way to manage; the most effective approach depends on the specific situation.  
- It rejects universal principles of management and instead focuses on adapting strategies to internal and external variables.  
- Management practices must be tailored to fit the unique needs, goals, and circumstances of each organization.  
- Organizational structure, leadership style, and decision-making processes should align with situational factors such as technology, environment, size, and culture.  
- Emphasizes flexibility and responsiveness to changing conditions.  
- Encourages managers to diagnose problems contextually before applying solutions.  
- Recognizes that different situations may require different management techniques for optimal performance.
